📖 Glossary & How EAC Works

Terms used in this app

BAC — Planned Hours (Budget at Completion)
The total hours that were originally planned/budgeted for that line item.
AC — Actual Hours
The hours that have actually been spent on that line item so far.
% Complete
Your honest estimate of how much of that line item's work is actually finished, as a percentage.
ETC — Estimate to Complete
A fresh, hands-on estimate of how many hours are still needed to finish the remaining work — given directly by whoever is doing the work, not calculated from a formula.
EV — Earned Hours (Earned Value)
The portion of the planned hours that your progress so far actually represents. It reflects how much value/progress has truly been "earned," not just how many hours were spent.
CPI — Cost Performance Index
A measure of efficiency: are you getting a full hour of progress for every hour spent? Below 1 means it's taking more hours than planned to make the same progress; above 1 means you're doing better than planned.
EAC — Estimate at Completion
The projected total hours that line item will take once it's fully finished — combining the hours already spent with a forecast of what's still needed.
Variance
The difference between EAC and BAC. Positive means you expect to end up over the original budget; zero or negative means on budget or under.

The three EAC methods, in plain English

Method 1 — "If we keep going at this pace"

This assumes that whatever speed or efficiency you've shown so far will continue for the rest of the work. Use it when slow (or fast) progress so far reflects a real, ongoing pattern — not a one-time fluke.

Method 2 — "Assume it was a one-time hiccup"

This assumes whatever caused the variance so far is behind you, and the rest of the work will go exactly according to the original plan from here on. Use it when you believe the issue has been fixed and won't repeat.

Method 3 — "Ask the team directly"

Instead of relying on a trend or formula, someone close to the work simply gives a fresh, bottom-up estimate of how many hours are left. This is usually the most accurate option when good first-hand judgment is available.

In this app, Method 3 is automatically used as the main EAC whenever an ETC value is entered for a row, since a human re-estimate is treated as more reliable. If ETC is left blank, the app falls back to Method 1, and if there isn't enough data yet for Method 1 (no actual hours logged), it falls back to Method 2.
